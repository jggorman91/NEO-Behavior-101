Effective Data Collection

Collecting objective data is the foundation of any effective behavior plan. It helps us move from "I feel like it's getting worse" to "The behavior occurs an average of 5 times per hour." Good data allows us to identify patterns, make informed decisions, and measure progress accurately.

Types of Data Collection

Frequency/Event Recording

Simply tallying the number of times a specific behavior occurs.
Best for: Behaviors that have a clear start and end, and don't happen so often that it's impossible to count (e.g., calling out, hitting, task completion).

Duration Recording

Measuring how long a behavior lasts, from start to finish.
Best for: Behaviors where the length of time is the primary concern (e.g., time spent out of seat, length of tantrums, time on-task).

Latency Recording

Measuring the time between a prompt/instruction and the beginning of the student's response.
Best for: Assessing compliance and processing speed (e.g., how long it takes a student to start their work after being told to do so).

Partial Interval Recording

You mark if the behavior occurred at any point during a short interval.
Best for: Behaviors you want to decrease. This method tends to overestimate behavior, making it a good choice for ensuring you catch every instance of a challenging behavior.

Whole Interval Recording

You mark if the behavior occurred for the entire duration of the interval.
Best for: Behaviors you want to increase, like "time on task" or "in-seat behavior." It sets a high bar for what is considered a success.

Momentary Time Sampling

You look at the student for just a moment at the end of an interval and record if the behavior is happening at that exact instant.
Best for: Classroom teachers who are also instructing, as it's less disruptive than continuous observation. It provides an estimate.

ABC Data (Antecedent-Behavior-Consequence)

A descriptive method where you record what happened right before the behavior, the behavior itself, and what happened right after.
Best for: Determining the function or "why" of a behavior. This is crucial for developing a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP).

Writing Effective Behavior & Executive Function IEP Goals

Crafting clear, measurable, and effective IEP goals is essential for guiding instruction and tracking student progress. Every goal must be grounded in the student's present levels of performance and meet SMART criteria.

Key Components of a Strong IEP Goal

A well-written goal starts with a strong foundation and follows a clear structure.

  • Present Levels (PLAAFP): The goal must be based on the student's current academic and functional abilities. This baseline data describes how the disability affects involvement in the general curriculum and is the starting point for any goal.
  • SMART Criteria: Goals must be Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, and Time-bound.
  • Three-Part Structure: A clear goal includes the Condition (when/where), the Target Behavior (what the student will do), and the Criterion (the measurable standard of success).
  • Progress Monitoring Plan: The IEP must specify how and how often progress will be measured (e.g., weekly frequency counts, daily checklists) and reported to parents.

Behavior & Executive Functioning Considerations

When writing goals for these areas, be specific about context, supports, and replacement behaviors.

  • Define behaviors in observable terms (e.g., instead of "impulse control," use "uses a taught strategy to pause and think before speaking").
  • Specify the supports the student will use (e.g., visual schedule, checklist, self-monitoring form, break card).
  • Focus on teaching a replacement behavior, not just reducing a problem behavior.
  • Use clear measures like frequency (number of times), duration (minutes on-task), or rating scales (self-rating of anxiety).

Examples of SMART IEP Goals

Elementary – ADHD (Impulse Control)

Given a visual cue and choice of movement strategies, [Student] will use a self‑calming strategy (e.g. deep breathing, squeeze ball) to pause before speaking in 4 out of 5 opportunities, as measured by teacher observation over 4 consecutive weeks.

Middle School – Autism (Emotional Regulation)

Given a break card and adult support, [Student] will use a coping strategy (e.g. request break, deep breathe) to reduce anxiety from 4/5 to ≤2/5 on a self-rating scale in 4 of 5 opportunities, over a 6-week period.

High School – ADHD (Executive Function)

Given a large project prompt, [Student] will outline the task into at least five sequential steps using a graphic organizer and submit the outline to the teacher by the next class period, with no more than 1 teacher prompt, on 4 out of 5 assignments.

Executive Functioning and Behavior Skills

Executive function (EF) skills are the mental processes that allow students to plan, organize, focus, remember, and manage multiple tasks. Strong EF and self-regulation are foundational for learning, and all students benefit from explicit instruction in these areas.

Key Skills to Teach

Organization & Planning: Breaking tasks into steps, using planners, and setting goals.

Time Management & Task Initiation: Scheduling work, using timers, and starting tasks promptly.

Working Memory & Focus: Holding multi-step directions in mind and attending to tasks.

Cognitive Flexibility: Shifting strategies when something isn’t working and finding alternative solutions.

Self-Monitoring & Reflection: Checking one's own work and adjusting strategies as needed.

Impulse Control & Inhibition: Thinking before acting, using self-talk and "stop signals."

Emotional Awareness & Regulation: Identifying feelings and using coping tools to stay calm.

Effective Teaching Strategies

  • Direct Instruction & Modeling: Explicitly teach each skill using think-alouds.
  • Visual Supports: Use graphic organizers, checklists, and color-coding.
  • Routines and Agendas: Build predictable routines to prime working memory.
  • Scaffolded Practice: Start with guidance and gradually release responsibility.
  • Integrate into Academics: Embed EF skill practice directly into lessons.
  • Positive Framing & Self-Talk: Encourage a positive mindset and motivational self-talk.

Utilizing Goalbook for Student Success

Goalbook is a powerful resource that empowers educators to design instruction based on the principles of Universal Design for Learning (UDL), ensuring that ALL students can succeed. It's a holistic toolkit for developing high-quality, standards-aligned IEPs and implementing them with effective, specially designed instruction.

Key Benefits of Using Goalbook

  • Saves Valuable Time: Quickly find research-based strategies, assessment resources, and goal statements instead of starting from scratch.
  • Improves IEP Quality: Develop measurable, standards-aligned goals that are truly individualized to student needs. Research shows goals written with Goalbook contain significantly more detail.
  • Promotes UDL: Access a rich library of strategies and resources categorized by UDL principles (Engagement, Representation, Action & Expression) to proactively address learning barriers.
  • Enhances Collaboration: Provides a common language and framework for general educators, special educators, and service providers to work together effectively.
  • Supports Data-Driven Decisions: Use assessment resources and probing questions to write specific, data-informed Present Levels statements.

How to Utilize Goalbook Effectively

  1. Define Present Levels: Start by exploring the assessment resources in Goalbook Toolkit. Use the standards-aligned items and reading passages to gather baseline data and write clear, data-driven Present Level statements that articulate student strengths and needs.
  2. Develop Standards-Aligned Goals: Use the Present Levels to search for aligned learning goals. Filter by subject, grade level, and standard. Select a goal and use the different levels of support (e.g., mild, moderate, intense) to customize it for your student.
  3. Design Differentiated Instruction: Once you have a goal, explore the linked UDL strategies. These strategies are designed to remove specific learning barriers. You can find ready-to-use resources, implementation tips, and printable materials to immediately apply in your classroom.
  4. Collaborate and Implement: Share the selected strategies and resources with the entire IEP team (paras, gen ed teachers). This ensures everyone is using the same language and providing consistent support, leading to higher fidelity of implementation.

Ensuring BIP Fidelity & Stakeholder Communication

A BIP is only effective if it's implemented consistently by everyone who works with the student. This is called fidelity. Clear communication and shared understanding are essential.

Key Steps for All Stakeholders

1. Ensure Access & Understanding

Every adult involved (gen ed teachers, paras, specials teachers, admin, bus drivers) must have access to the BIP. Don't assume they've read it just because it's in the IEP. Review it with them.

2. Clarify Roles

Each person should know their specific role. Who is prompting the replacement behavior? Who provides the reinforcement? Who takes the data? Be explicit.

3. Utilize Technology for Accountability

Systems like Euna can be valuable. When you email a BIP, you can often see if the email has been opened. This adds a layer of accountability and is a good starting point for a follow-up conversation: "I saw you had a chance to open the BIP, do you have any questions?"

4. Schedule In-Person Reviews

Technology is a tool, not a replacement for conversation. Hold brief, in-person meetings or check-ins at the start of implementation and periodically thereafter to troubleshoot and ensure everyone is on the same page.

Front-Line Behavior Strategies

These are proactive and reactive strategies that can be used in the classroom to support positive behavior. Many of these are excellent Tier 1 supports for all students.

Token Boards

How to Implement:

A visual system where a student earns tokens for desired behaviors, which can be exchanged for a larger reward. Deliver tokens immediately with specific praise.

First-Then Boards

How to Implement:

A simple visual showing a non-preferred task ("First") followed by a preferred activity ("Then"). Be consistent and follow through immediately.

Visual Schedules

How to Implement:

Use pictures or words to show the sequence of activities for the day or a specific task. Reduces anxiety and makes the environment predictable.

Social Stories

How to Implement:

Read a short, descriptive story that explains a social situation, skill, or concept to a student proactively before the situation occurs.

Routines & Procedures

How to Implement:

Explicitly teach, model, and practice classroom routines (e.g., entering the room, turning in work) to make expectations clear and predictable.

Opportunities to Respond

How to Implement:

Increase the rate at which students actively respond (e.g., using choral responses, whiteboards, think-pair-share) to boost engagement.

4-to-1 Ratio

How to Implement:

Provide at least four positive interactions or specific praises for every one correction or redirection to build a positive classroom climate.

Scheduled Breaks

How to Implement:

Proactively provide scheduled breaks (using a timer) to prevent overwhelm, rather than waiting for a student to be dysregulated.

When a BIP Isn't Enough: Getting More Support

Sometimes, despite a well-written BIP and high-fidelity implementation, a student may not make the expected progress. This is not a failure, but a sign that more support is needed.

The Importance of Fidelity FIRST

Before escalating, we must be confident that the current plan has been implemented with fidelity. Ask these questions:

  • Is the data being taken consistently and accurately?
  • Are all staff members using the same strategies and language?
  • Are the reinforcements being delivered as planned and are they still motivating for the student?
  • Has the environment changed in a way that might be impacting the behavior?

The Escalation Pathway

If you have solid data showing the plan is being implemented with fidelity but the behavior is not improving (or is getting worse), it's time to act. Don't wait.

1

First Point of Contact: School Psychologist

Gather your data and the BIP. Contact your building's school psychologist. They are your primary partner in this process. They will help you analyze the situation, review the data, and observe the student.

2

Team Problem-Solving

The school psychologist will likely convene a team meeting (including you, the general education teahcer, Admin, and other relevant staff) to review the data and fidelity checks. The team may decide to adjust the BIP with new strategies or goals.

3

Considering District-Level Support

If the team has exhausted its resources and the student is still not making progress, the school psychologist will navigate the process to determine if more intensive, district-level behavior support is warranted. This is a collaborative decision based on extensive data and documentation.
